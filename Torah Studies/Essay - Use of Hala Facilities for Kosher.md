## Blood Removal: Halal practices aligning with the original Torah instructions 

One of the primary concerns in **Torah-based slaughter** is the complete **removal of blood**, as commanded in **Leviticus 17:10-14** and **Deuteronomy 12:16**. The Torah instructs that **blood must be poured onto the ground**, ensuring that it is not consumed. This divine commandent reflects how Hashem intended for the blood to be removed. Thus, highlighting its sanctity and symbolizing the separation of life from the flesh.

Certain halal procedures (such as blood drainage and knife sharpness) approximate Torah principles, but others diverge significantly. Therefore, only a Torah-observant Karaite or trusted trained individual overseeing the process can validate its permissibility.

**Karaites**, do not follow protocols such as **salting and soaking meat**.
This practice was introduced to extract residual blood by rabbinites. However, Karaites rely on the Torah’s instruction for blood to be poured out at the time of slaughter, not through post-slaughter processing (Leviticus 17:13).

## To Ensure Compatibility with Torah-Based Dietary Laws  

For a **halal facility** to meet **Karaite dietary standards**, several factors must be verified:  
- **Animal Selection** – The Torah specifies that only animals that **chew cud and have split hooves** are permitted (**Leviticus 11:3**). While halal slaughter allows **camels and rabbits**, these species are **rarely processed in Western halal facilities**, making verification easier.  
- **Cross-Contamination Prevention** – If a facility processes non-kosher animals, such as pigs, camels, or rabbits. Then, the entire operation becomes as complex as that of a standard slaughterhouse handling those species. This means that both the facility and its equipment must undergo thorough sanitation before they can host a kosher slaughter, a requirement which is generally not feasible for communities with limited kosher adherence.
- **Processing Standards & Karaite Oversight** – A **Karaite Jew** would ideally oversee the entire slaughter and processing procedure to verify that no visible or internal defects are present that could invalidate the animal's kosher status. If it is determined that the animal would have died naturally without the slaughter, then the meat cannot be considered kosher.


## Sourcing & Inspection of Animals  

Unlike **halal slaughter**, where pregnant animals could be **lawfully processed**, Karaite slaughter requires **careful sourcing and inspection** **before slaughter** to ensure compliance with **Torah laws**.  

### **Key Requirements for Karaite Animal Selection**  
- **Inspection Before Slaughter** – A trained individual must **examine the animal** before slaughter to ensure it is **healthy, fit, and not pregnant**.  
- **Avoiding Pregnant Animals** – Unlike certain Muslims, Karaites reject the slaughter of pregnant animals, not due to an explicit command, but based on broader Torah values regarding reproductive life and maternal protection reflected in Deut. 22:6–7 (bird and nest), and Lev. 22:28 (mother and young).

- **Inspection After Slaughter** – A trained individual must **examine the animal's organs** after slaughter to ensure it was **healthy and free of diseases**.  
 
## The Importance of Proper Slaughter Knives in Karaite Practice  

- **Sharpness & Precision** – The knife must be **exceptionally sharp** to ensure an **instantaneous and painless** cut, minimizing suffering.  
- **Size & Material** – Ideally, the knife should be **long enough** to perform a clean cut **in a single stroke**, reducing unnecessary distress.  
- **Smooth Blade** – The blade must be **free of nicks or serrations**, ensuring a **clean and effective** cut without tearing flesh.  

While the Torah does not explicitly prescribe blade type, commandments such as ‘You shall not muzzle an ox when it treads out the grain’ (Deut. 25:4) and ‘The righteous person regards the life of his beast’ (Prov. 12:10) imply that minimizing animal suffering is a Torah value. Thus, a smooth, flawless blade is inferred to uphold the Torah’s principle of compassion in slaughter.

I have also been told in discussion with some muslim relatives that some Halal facilities rely on mechanized systems, where a simple button press by a Muslim operator initiates the slaughter process. 

For me personally, Even if the mechanical blade were technically sufficient, the automated and impersonal nature of such slaughter violates the Torah's implied ethic of human accountability and reverence for life.

### **Karaite Oversight in Knife Preparation**  

To ensure Torah alignment, Karaites should provide clear instruction regarding knife standards, and ideally bring their own. While Torah law does not mandate who provides the knife, doing so gives assurance that tools meet required compassion and sharpness standards.


Since **halal slaughter** follows **Islamic regulations**, the knives used in these facilities may not align with **Karaite scriptural expectations**.Therefore, by providing the knife, Karaites can ensure that the slaughter meets **Torah standards** while utilizing **halal facilities**.

## Moreover, Mutual Benefit for Forbidden Parts

One of the lesser-discussed but critical Torah mandates concerns the prohibition against consuming certain fats (chelev) and the sciatic nerve (gid hanasheh), as stated in Leviticus 3:17 and Genesis 32:32. These parts are not optional preferences. They are forbidden. In some kosher operations, these portions are removed and then sold to non-Jews for profit—yet the Jewish consumer still pays two, three, or even four times the actual value of the animal.

I do not accept this approach. To me, it violates both the letter and the spirit of Torah. Specifically, Leviticus 25:14:

> *"When you sell anything to your neighbor or buy from your neighbor, do not wrong one another."*

Inflating prices on kosher status grounds crosses that line. Likewise, Leviticus 19:35–36 forbids dishonest weights and measures, and Deuteronomy 25:13–16 warns against unfair standards in trade. 

When kosher becomes a vehicle for profit on top of an industry being lockdown by rabinnite certifications at the expense of fellow Jews fulfilling commandments, it ceases to be righteous observance, it becomes a form of exploitation.

Worse still, the “kosher meat” being sold at three times its value may not even be kosher at all. As Isaiah 66:3 warns:

“He who slaughters an ox is like one who kills a man…”

This verse emphasizes the spiritual and moral gravity of the act of slaughter. In Karaite tradition, it teaches that if a slaughterer lacks integrity or reverence, the slaughter is not only spiritually flawed, it renders the meat invalid, even if technically correct.

Given the behavior of the kosher industry today—marked by commercialism, opacity, and exclusion—it is not a stretch to doubt the spiritual validity of its output.

Back on Topic, Removing the sciatic nerve is highly technical. I do not yet have the expertise, and I cannot entrust this delicate task to just anyone. Even many Muslim butchers, though skilled in halal slaughter, do not remove the nerve since it is not part of their law. This leaves us with a few Torah-aligned paths.

### Torah-Respecting Alternatives

The first and best path is to **learn from a qualified Karaite** how to remove these forbidden parts properly. If that is not possible, the second option is to **give them away** as permitted in **Deuteronomy 14:21**, which says:

> *"You shall not eat anything that dies of itself. You may give it to the stranger who is within your gates, that he may eat it, or you may sell it to a foreigner. For you are a holy people to the Lord your God."*

This text shows that it is permitted to sell or give certain parts to non-Israelites, and I acknowledge that permission. However, from my personal understanding, the Torah’s repeated call to righteousness warns us that profiting from what is forbidden to us, even when technically permitted, can lead us down a slippery slope of spiritual compromise or selective segragation of commandments such as loopholes often exploited by Tamuld.

It is understandable that losing part of the animal causes the cost of usable meat to rise, but there is a **difference between managing a loss and profiting off what is forbidden** but we should not be punished with unfair makups for our faith.
